We had a long playtest session with my cousin Lauren and uncle Arran. They are regular board/card gamers and Lauren especially is reading and understanding rules. 
Started getting better cards made, printed the PnP set on card, cut them out, laminated, and rounded the corners.

Started with Trigon. There was initial confusion on how the grid is defined. Placing the first card, there was no way of telling whether it was a centre, edge or corner. We have all played a game called Instinkt where you take turns placing cards to create a 4x4 grid, in that game each card played has to be diagonally or orthogonally adjacent to a card already on the table. You can build in any direction as long as it would theoretically fit within the bounds of a 4x4 grid so, what you think might be a card placed in the centre, could end up as a corner or edge. Without a way of fixing the 9 positions of the grid at the start of the game, it was easy to slip into our Instinkt habit of unfixed grid play.
The rules were left open on my laptop. Every single turn, every player was checking the requirements and abilities of the symbols on both sides of the card. This made turns very slow. Lauren suggested a reference card for each player but I wasn't keen on the idea of adding extra components that only work for specific games. The mnemonics were initially read but not used to remember requirements/abilities. 
There was also confusion on whether the symbol requirements were the only way a card could be placed or if it was just to activate the abilities. We played as a requirement to use the ability. 
A generic feedback note is finding a way to distinguish between a draw deck and discard pile.
We sometimes forgot to make use of chaining abilities, I think this was a first time play issue and would be better in a second playthrough. It didn't become a genuine issue but we all felt like multi-chaining could become overwhelming. If you change two cards (A and B) and both requirements are met, what is the order/limits of chaining? Do you follow the chain of A all the way through before starting with B, can you switch between the two chains? If the ability on one side of a card is used, can the other side's ability be used in the same turn?
Most turns I felt like I wasn't playing with a strategy, unless I was set up for an obvious and direct capture, I was placing cards arbitrarily or at least placing it to activate the ability with no real goal in mind. I would hope this would improve with multiple games.

We played Janus next. Used three gates. The face down side of gates were constantly being checked by all players. When playing a card, we didn't declare whether we played it "my face" or "your face", we just placed it next to the gate and allowed everyone to examine both sides whenever they wanted. Drawing new cards from the deck was an issue because everyone had seen one symbol on the top card. We tried to fix this by drawing from the bottom but this gave two issues; holding your cards in one hand (keeping one side facing you and the other to the other players) whilst also picking up the deck and secretly adding the bottom card to your hand was physically very challenging. It also didn't fix one side of the final card being visible to all players; we hid the top of the deck with a leftover crown card as a temporary fix here. For terminology, we fell into calling the initial three cards "locks" and we had to unlock the locks by playing a connected key of two cards. The "omens" were just called "clues". We played three round of this. The first two ended with us needing a key which was already played (either as a starting lock or a key played somewhere else). This didn't feel like a bug in the game but that we should've been more careful with which keys to use. This would've been extra challenging if we didn't have full visibility of all played cards. I'm not sure if it would have been too challenging or even impossible using my face/your face rule.

One round of blight next. Lauren read the rules and said they were very clear and easy to understand. It was more the type of game she was expecting for this deck than the previous games. Play went through without any issues. We had an unexpected outcome with Arran having around 6 of the 9/crown cards in his hand and ending up with the least points by far. This was due to him sloughing most of them by not being able to follow suit. Lauren didn't enjoy the game because she felt like she had a bad hand and couldn't do anything to recover from it. Arran and I disagreed and said this is typical of most trick-taking games but this gives even more opportunities to recover by having two sides of each card as opportunity to play. The only other issue was having so many cards in each players hand. Starting with 12 cards and having to check both sides was a lot to manage. If player 1 leads with a moon, the other players have to check both sides for all moon cards, align them so they face the same way, then decide which one to play. Because there is no natural ordering to the cards, this has to be done every turn.

We finished with crossroads. Initially, we started with Arran and Lauren playing with me observing. From the start, they didn't enjoy it. 14 cards is too many, especially having to check both sides. They felt like they should have been able to make a plan but there were too many options and didn't know how to start. When I mentioned being able to flip roads, they said it was already too overwhelming and that would add even more confusion. We tried switching to 3 players with 9 cards each and using the leftover card as the first road. This felt a lot more manageable and we played through until two of us declared we wanted to stop and Arran took one last turn. Deciding how to end play was still undefined. We suggested if one player wanted to stop, the others could take one more turn but this still had the issue of a player stopping early if they make two quick contracts. One idea was only being allowed to stop if your hand only consisted of completed contracts. Once we had stopped, since we all made our contracts and I was the only player to pass (not using one of my cards for a road), I won. We thought that my pass gave me the advantage. Based on this, we tested again with me passing every single turn just to see the outcome. By the end of the game, Lauren noted that every route was possible to make. This gave me an overwhelming win without doing anything. We brainstormed some ways to fix this and Lauren suggested either fixing some cards as contracts and some as roads directly after the deal, or pre-declaring which direction your contract had to go. The second option didn't make sense to me; when and how do you decide and commit this? The first didn't feel as neat for some reason.

General issues were managing large hand sizes, finding ways to hide the backs of cards from other players especially when flipping them around in your hand, one symbol of the top card of the deck always being visible to all players (issue for some games, useful for others), occasionally confusing discard pile with the drawing deck.
