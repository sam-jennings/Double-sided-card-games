# Art Direction and Illustration

## TL;DR
- DO NOT use AI art in finished games — causes major backlash, boycotts, reputation damage. OK for early prototyping only.
- Art process: get 2-3 samples → find artist → iterate sketches → use approved style as baseline → colour versions → graphic designer adds elements
- Standard art pricing: Premium $500, Standard $200-300, Simple $100-150, Black & White $150 or less, Box art $2,000-5,000
- Finding artists: BoardGameGeek credits, ArtStation (searchable by style), social media, game websites
- Working collaboratively: clear descriptions, allow creative freedom, compliment their work, add 2-3 month buffer to deadlines
- Kill fee contracts (typically 50%) protect both parties; pay promptly — reputation matters
- If pitching to publishers: DON'T commission finished art. Mood board sufficient. Publishers want their own art direction.
- Idea theft more likely after publication success than during pitch; don't be overly protective of early concepts

## Core Concepts

- **Art Style Questions** — How serious is theme? How serious is gameplay? What emotions do you want players feeling? Answers guide artistic tone selection.

- **Mood Board** — Intentionally flexible direction-setter. Establishes artistic vision without committing to specifics. Used for communicating style to collaborators and publishers.

- **Kill Fee Contract** — Clause (typically 50%) protecting both parties if project terminates partway through. Allows designer to exit if direction isn't working without full payment; shows respect for artist's time.

- **Black Plate Replacement** — Printing efficiency technique where text prints on separate plate per language. All cards print without text, then text plate prints separately for each language.

- **Idea Theft Reality** — Ideas are easy; execution is hard. Most theft happens after publication success (counterfeits, knockoffs), not during pitch stage. Don't be overly protective of early concepts.

- **Automated Opponent Deck** — 5-10 themed cards per enemy type specifying that enemy's turn actions. Provides variety and thematic consistency for solo competitive modes.

## Design Principles

- If you have too many art ideas at once → make a decision (even temporary), test it, iterate based on results (better than endless deliberation)
- If you're pitching to a publisher → don't commission finished art; mood board sufficient (publishers want their own art direction)
- If you want final approval on visuals → self-publish (full control) or explicitly state in pitch: "Final approval on all visual elements" (may scare publishers away)
- If artist misses deadline → added 2-3 month buffer is insurance; don't give artists your hard deadline
- Avoid micromanaging artists → collaborative mindset produces better work; clear descriptions + creative freedom
- Never put text on dice/tokens → text translation per language is too expensive; use icons instead
- Reduce art requirements strategically → text-heavy cards don't need illustrations, reuse generic artwork, reserve art for key components

## Design Frameworks

**Art Process Framework**:
1. Collect 2-3 initial art samples to establish style direction
2. Find artist whose style resonates
3. Create mood/aesthetic brief
4. Request pencil sketches; iterate until direction is locked
5. Approve final style as baseline for all future artwork
6. Commission colour versions
7. Graphic designer adds typography, icons, design elements on top
8. Final revisions before printing

**Art Style Decision Framework**:
- How serious is the theme? (Dark Batman vs. Silly Batman — both valid)
- How serious is the gameplay? (Matches or contrasts with art tone)
- What emotions do players need to feel? (This guides artistic tone)

**Finding Artists Framework**:
1. BoardGameGeek: Credits list on games you like
2. ArtStation: Filter by art style (search "troll" to see different artist interpretations)
3. Social media: Instagram portfolios (less searchable but visible)
4. Game websites: Check credits of games with appealing art

**Collaborative Artist Relationship Framework**:
- Collaborative mindset: treat as creative partner, not servant
- Clear descriptions: highlight gameplay requirements and character references, allow compositional freedom
- Give compliments: acknowledge their talent when they nail something
- Show references: share artwork that inspires your vision, even if by others
- Add buffer: always add 2-3 months to stated deadline
- Pay promptly: reputation matters in artist community

**Kill Fee Contract Framework**:
- Include clause for terminating partway through
- Typically 50% of contracted work
- Protects both parties
- Shows respect for artist's time investment
- Build into contract from start

**Spec Requirements for Artists**:
- Dimensions: size artwork will be printed at
- Resolution: 300 DPI (dots per inch) for print
- Bleed: usually 0.25" (quarter-inch) trimming allowance on edges
- File format: TIFF or high-quality PDF
- Colour mode: RGB or CMYK (depends on printer)

**Reducing Art Requirements Framework**:
- Text-heavy cards don't need illustrations
- Generic tokens can use simple geometric shapes
- Duplicated elements reuse same artwork
- Reserve art for key components only
- Example: Dungeon crawl 50 cards = 6 character cards + 3-4 boss enemies get art, skills/abilities are text-only, generic tokens reused

**Automated Opponent Deck Framework** (for solo modes):
- Create 5-10 themed cards per enemy type
- Each card specifies what that enemy does this turn
- Use theme to guide behaviour (sniper: move away, then shoot from distance)
- Provides variety and thematic consistency

## Common Pitfalls

- **AI art in finished games**: Major backlash, boycotts, reputation damage → use only for early prototyping
- **Over-protecting early ideas**: Ideas are easy; execution is hard → don't be paranoid about theft during pitch stage
- **Commissioning finished art for publisher pitch**: Publishers want their own art direction; you waste money on art they ignore → mood board sufficient
- **Micromanaging artists**: Produces worse work; reduces willingness to work with you again → collaborative mindset, clear briefs, creative freedom
- **Giving artists your hard deadline**: They miss it, you panic → add 2-3 month buffer; give them earlier deadline
- **Contradictory feedback on art style**: One person loves it, another hates it → less actionable than consistent feedback; test your preferred style thoroughly
- **Changing specifications mid-project**: Artist had to redo work; morale drops, quality suffers → clear specs upfront
- **Late payment**: Damages reputation in artist community → impacts future availability and willingness to work with you
- **Text on dice/tokens for international**: Each language requires separate production → use icons for international games
- **Too many art requirements**: Budget overspend, timeline issues → strategically reduce: text-heavy cards, reuse generic artwork, reserve for key components
- **No clear style direction**: Artist has nothing to build from → provide mood board, references, clear emotional goals

## Application

**When starting art direction**: Collect 2-3 samples that resonate with your vision. Ask key questions: how serious is theme? how serious is gameplay? what emotions do players need? Use answers to identify artist style. Get sketches, iterate until locked, use approved style as baseline for all future art.

**When finding an artist**: Check BoardGameGeek credits (who created art on games you love), search ArtStation by style (type "troll" to see different interpretations), check social media portfolios. Direct contact via portfolio website with pitch + why they're perfect + sample work they've done + full list of needed art.

**When working with artists**: Be collaborative. Write clear descriptions (gameplay requirements, character references) but allow compositing freedom. Give compliments when they nail something. Show reference artwork by others. Add 2-3 month buffer to deadline; don't give them your hard deadline. Pay promptly — reputation matters in artist community.

**When reducing art budget**: Don't commission art for text-heavy cards. Reuse generic artwork across multiple components. Reserve unique art for key components only (character cards, boss enemies, cover). Example: dungeon crawl 50-card game could use art for 6 character cards + 3-4 boss enemies, text-only skills/abilities, generic token artwork.

**When pitching to publishers**: Mood board only, not finished art. Publishers want their own art direction. Finished art wastes your money and locks them into your vision. If visual control is important, self-publish or explicitly state "Final approval on all visual elements" in pitch (may scare publishers away).

**When AI art temptation arises**: Don't. Use for early prototyping to explore ideas, but finished games need human art. Community backlash, boycotts, and reputation damage aren't worth the cost savings.

**When managing versions**: Version individual components (game_board_v4, cards_v6), keep old files for reference, create dated playtesting notes with bold issues and strikethrough fixes. Don't constantly update rulebook during early development. Avoid wasting time on constantly changing rules.

**When managing file handoff to graphic designer**: Provide component specs (size, usage, unusual requirements), reference artwork showing desired direction, list of all components needing design. Graphic designer will add typography, icons, design elements on top of your approved artwork.

**Tradeoff**: Realistic detailed art is expensive ($500/card) but visually striking. Simpler styles (sketches, black & white) are cheaper ($100-150) but less impressive. Match art budget to component count and visual impact goals.
