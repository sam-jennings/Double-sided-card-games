# Lesson 15: Bringing It All Together

## TL;DR
- Final project: three components max 5 pages: Pitch Sheet (1-2pp), Theme & Look (2pp), Business Plan (2pp)
- Pitch sheet is your actual deliverable to publishers; Theme & Look is internal vision document; Business Plan proves market awareness
- Market analysis: research BGG (sort by ratings), identify successful competitor games, find market gaps, list 3-5 target publishers
- Production estimates: fixed costs + (one-time costs ÷ print run) = per-unit cost → ×5 for MSRP
- Print runs: KS indie 1,000-2,000, publishers 5,000+. Work backward: desired MSRP ÷ 5 = required per-unit cost
- Five-page limit is deliberate constraint to force clarity. Length discipline is the skill.
- What publishers want: clarity of vision, realistic assessment, market awareness, professional presentation, passion
- Mood/atmosphere and art style are separate decisions: dark + cartoony is valid. Distinguish both explicitly.

## Core Concepts
- **The Final Project as Professional Template** — Three integrated sections that together form a submission-ready package. Pitch sheet is reusable for every future publisher pitch; Theme & Look is your internal design north star; Business Plan proves market viability. This structure is your career template.

- **Market Analysis as Competitive Validation** — Don't research to prove your idea is unique (it's not). Research to prove: (1) there's a market for this type of game, (2) successful precedents exist (players want it), (3) gaps exist where your game fits, (4) specific publishers publish similar games. This anchors your pitch in reality.

- **Backward-Working from Target MSRP** — If you want $40 MSRP, divide by 5 = $8 per-unit cost target. Now you can assess: can my components be manufactured for $8? If not, either raise MSRP, cut components, or increase print run to amortize one-time costs. This forces feasibility reality-checking.

- **Mood vs. Art Style Distinction** — Mood is the emotional tone (dark, whimsical, gritty, elegant). Art style is the visual technique (cartoony, realistic, watercolor, vector, pixel). These are independent choices. Explicitly stating both prevents ambiguity and signals sophisticated thinking.

- **Length Constraint as Design Discipline** — Five pages max forces prioritization. You cannot include everything. Distilling to essentials reveals what's actually important. Publishers value brevity; it shows you understand their time and your game's core value.

## Design Principles
- If writing more than 5 pages → cut ruthlessly; prioritize Pitch Sheet > Business Plan > Theme & Look
- If unclear which elements are most important → trust the Pitch Sheet structure from Lesson 13; lead with Who/What/How You Win
- Mood and art style should be explicitly stated separately → don't bury mood in art references
- Market analysis should reference real, successful games → use BGG search and ratings, not just "games like X exist"
- Timeline must be specific (dates, publisher targets, prototype milestones) → vague timelines signal uncertainty
- Print run estimate must align with business model → KS indie 1,000-2,000, publisher 5,000+, choose one
- If no working prototype exists → still acceptable; describe "dream game" end state. Showing commitment matters more.

## Heuristics (Fast Decision Rules)
- **The 5-Page Test:** If you're over 5 pages, reread. Every sentence must justify its space. Cut aggressively.
- **The "Why This Publisher?" Rule:** Can you name 3-5 specific publishers and explain why each fits? If not, business plan is incomplete.
- **The BGG Reality Check:** Search your game's category on BoardGameGeek, sort by rating. What's the top game? Is your game better? Different? How?
- **The Backward Math:** Desired MSRP ÷ 5 = required per-unit cost. Can you hit that with your components? If not, adjust now.
- **The Mood + Style Check:** Can you describe mood in one sentence AND art style in one sentence separately? If they blur together, rewrite with distinction.

## Design Frameworks
- **Final Project Structure (5-page maximum, strict):**
  - Pitch Sheet (1-2 pages): Game name, theme, overview, Big Three stats, components list, USP, victory condition
  - Theme & Look (1-2 pages): Mood statement, art direction, sample images/mood board, visual tone, thematic pillars
  - Business Plan (1-2 pages): Market analysis (3-5 competitor games + why different, 3-5 target publishers + why fit), production budget summary (fixed cost, one-time cost, print run, MSRP), next steps timeline (specific dates, prototype milestones, publisher outreach/crowdfunding launch)

- **Market Analysis Template:**
  ```
  Competitor Games (found via BGG search):
  - [Game Name] — Why successful, how it differs from mine, what mine improves
  - [repeating for 3-5 games]
  
  Target Publishers:
  - [Publisher Name] — Recent games they publish, why my game fits, contact method
  - [repeating for 3-5 publishers]
  
  Market Gap: [One sentence on why this game fills a need]
  ```

- **Production Budget Summary (one-page):**
  ```
  Fixed Costs per Unit: $[X]
  One-Time Costs: $[Y]
  Print Run: [Z] copies
  Amortized One-Time: $[Y÷Z]
  Total per Unit: $[X + (Y÷Z)]
  MSRP (×5): $[total × 5]
  ```

## Common Pitfalls
- **Exceeding 5 pages** → Why fails: Shows inability to prioritize; confuses readers. How to fix: Cut Theme & Look detail first, then Business Plan detail, preserve Pitch Sheet. Every sentence must earn its space.

- **Vague next steps timeline** → Why fails: Publishers see "we'll pitch eventually" as lack of conviction. How to fix: Write specific dates: "Prototype complete by March 15th. Publisher outreach April 1-30th. Campaign launch if needed June 1st."

- **Market analysis lists no real games** → Why fails: Suggests no market research; publishers assume you don't understand competition. How to fix: Search BGG, name specific successful games, explain how yours is different/better/addresses gaps.

- **No specific target publishers** → Why fails: Shows you haven't researched who publishes games like yours. How to fix: List 3-5 real publishers (by name), list their recent games that match your game type, explain why your game fits each one.

- **Treating mood and art style as the same thing** → Why fails: Signals unsophisticated visual thinking. "Dark fantasy art" is vague; "dark mood + cartoony art style" is clear. How to fix: State mood independently (mood: whimsical, art: realistic). Both should be explicit.

- **Including prototype if it's non-functional** → Why fails: Weak prototype hurts pitch more than no prototype. How to fix: If prototype isn't polished, describe your "dream game" instead. Focus on concepts, not execution.

- **Unrealistic MSRP** → Why fails: Publisher immediately knows you haven't done real budgeting. How to fix: Work backward from components to per-unit cost. Use ×5 multiplier. If MSRP is too high or low, adjust components or print run.

## Application
- **When writing market analysis section:** Search BoardGameGeek for similar games. Sort by rating to find truly successful ones. Name them. Explain why yours is different. "My game is like Wingspan but for bees" is not analysis; "Wingspan proved theme-focused games with quality production sell well. My game applies that model to insect taxonomy, addressing feedback that Wingspan's scope was limited to birds" is analysis.

- **When selecting target publishers:** Research their recent releases (last 2 years). Do they publish your game's category? Do they publish games at your target MSRP? Have they published games with similar complexity? If "no" to two or more, remove from list. Precision signals seriousness.

- **When writing next steps:** Be specific. "Q2 2026: Prototype complete. May 2026: Submit to Fantasy Flight. July 2026: Crowdfunding campaign launch if no publisher interest." Specific timelines show you've thought through the path.

- **Tradeoff: Detail vs. Brevity** → Five pages is constraint, not suggestion. Every detail must serve the pitch. Theme & Look can be cut to images + one-sentence mood/style if space tight. Business Plan can be one page if production numbers are simple.

- **Tradeoff: Prototype vs. Concept** → Weak/unfinished prototype hurts more than none. If prototype isn't presentation-ready, describe dream game concept instead. Corey will ask "is this playable?" and you should be honest. Concept-only pitches are acceptable; broken prototypes are not.

- **Example:** A deck-building game targeting $30 MSRP: per-unit cost must be $6. (200 cards + rules + box likely $3-4 fixed; $8K art ÷ 5,000 = $1.60 one-time; total $4.60, leaves margin). Target publishers: AEG (deck builders), Cryptozoic (component-heavy), Wizkids (category match). Market gap: "Deck builders with asymmetric powers but no randomized power draws." Next step: "Prototype complete February, submit AEG/Cryptozoic by April."
