# Lesson 13: The Art of the Board Game Pitch

## TL;DR
- Pitch sheet is your game's CV: one page, three functions (inform, excite, cost-estimate)
- Elevator pitch test: can someone relay your game accurately after hearing it? (Telephone game test)
- The Big Three: players (range), ages (range), playtime (range) + USP
- Three core questions every pitch must answer: Who Are You (player role)? What Do You Do (actions)? How Do You Win (victory condition)?
- ABC principle: Accuracy (truthful), Brevity (relay-able), Clarity (no jargon)
- Component list precision directly signals to publishers production feasibility, art needs, packaging, manufacturer capability
- Blend narrative and mechanics—people remember stories and moments, not rule lists
- Customize format and depth for your target audience (kids' games = large fonts; war games = difficulty ratings)

## Core Concepts
- **Elevator Pitch Test** — Can a person who hears your pitch explain the game accurately to someone else? This "telephone game" test reveals whether your core messaging is clear and complete enough to survive retelling. Failure means your pitch is missing essential info or uses unclear language.

- **Pitch Sheet as Dual Document** — Serves two purposes simultaneously: (1) practical evaluation tool for publishers (Is this feasible? What does it cost?), (2) promotional tool for players and stakeholders (Why should anyone care?). These purposes sometimes tension; balance both.

- **Component List Precision** — Specific ranges ("50-60 action cards" not "many cards") signal professional understanding. Publishers immediately calculate: art complexity, assembly time, packaging requirements, manufacturer capability match, production cost accuracy. Vague component lists suggest amateurism.

- **The Big Three Stats** — Player count, age range, and playtime ALL must be ranges (e.g., "2-4 players," "12+," "45-60 min"), never fixed numbers. Ranges acknowledge manufacturing variability and player experience variance. Single numbers look either uninformed or restrictive.

- **Spice (Presentation)** — Clean, readable format with sample art or mood board. Visual appeal doesn't replace content but dramatically increases engagement and memorability. Publishers receive dozens of pitches; yours must stand out visually while staying professional.

## Design Principles
- If your pitch can't be relayed by a listener → rewrite for clarity and completeness
- If your component list is vague → replace with specific ranges (e.g., "30-40 wooden tokens" not "lots of pieces")
- If your pitch includes jargon → translate to plain language or remove it entirely
- Avoid commissioning original art for the pitch sheet unless you have an existing artist relationship
- Prioritize narrative + mechanical description over mechanics-only explanations; people retain stories better
- Customize format for your target publisher's catalog: children's games need large fonts, war games need difficulty/complexity ratings
- Apply accuracy over optimism: better to underpromise on component quality and overdeliver than vice versa
- Don't bury your USP; it should be clear within the first sentence or two

## Heuristics (Fast Decision Rules)
- **The Relay Test:** Read your pitch to someone unfamiliar with it, then ask them to explain it to a third person. If they fail, rewrite.
- **The Three Questions Rule:** Your pitch must directly answer: Who am I? What do I do? How do I win? If someone reads it and can't answer all three, it's incomplete.
- **The Jargon Check:** If you're using gaming terms, is it because they're precise or because they're default? If the latter, translate.
- **The Big Three Must Be Ranges:** Player count, age, playtime—all ranges. Single numbers are red flags.
- **Component Specificity Signal:** If you can't name specific component counts, you haven't playtested enough.

## Design Frameworks
- **Pitch Sheet Structure (One-Page Template):**
  1. Title + Visual Hook (top third): Game name, theme, one striking image/mood
  2. Game Overview (middle third): One paragraph blending theme and core gameplay loop
  3. The Big Three + Components (practical section): Player count, ages, time, specific components list
  4. Victory Condition + USP (bottom third): How you win, what's special about this game
  
- **The ABC Verification Checklist:**
  - Accuracy: Is every claim factual and tested? Are stats based on playtest data?
  - Brevity: Can a literate person read this in 3-5 minutes? Could they relay it?
  - Clarity: Are there unfamiliar terms? Can a non-gamer understand this?

- **Component List Format:**
  ```
  Materials:
  - [X-Y quantity] [component type] + description if special
  - Example: "50-60 action cards (two-sided, poker sized)"
  - Example: "1 punchboard (35 standees, mounted on chipboard)"
  ```

## Common Pitfalls
- **Pitch too mechanical, no narrative** → Why fails: Players don't connect to systems; they connect to moments and stories. Dry rule explanations bore publishers. How to fix: Lead with theme/moment, then explain mechanics that create it. ("In Deadman's Hand, showdowns create tense standoffs because..." not "This game uses a simultaneous action selection mechanic...")

- **Component list too vague** → Why fails: Publishers can't estimate costs, can't assess feasibility, assume inexperience. How to fix: Playtest, count everything, provide ranges. "Lots of tokens" → "300-350 wooden tokens, 5mm diameter, two colors."

- **Big Three stats as single numbers** → Why fails: Looks inflexible, suggests no iteration for different player counts. How to fix: Always use ranges (2-5 players, 8+, 30-45 min). This also acknowledges reality: playtime varies.

- **Over-designed pitch sheet** → Why fails: Expensive, amateurish if art isn't professional, distracts from content. How to fix: Clean layout, one good mood image, readable fonts. Professional presentation beats flashy.

- **Burying the USP** → Why fails: Publishers decide in first 10 seconds if they're interested; if your unique element isn't visible, they don't find it. How to fix: "This is [theme] + [core mechanic that's special]." Front-load it.

- **Jargon without translation** → Why fails: Not all publishers/players know "worker placement," "push-your-luck," "tableau building." Clarity fails. How to fix: Describe the action in plain terms: "On your turn, you send your workers to locations to gather resources" not "This is a worker placement game."

## Connections
- **Lesson 14 (The Business of Board Games):** Component list precision feeds directly into production cost estimates. Vague pitches lead to inaccurate budgets.
- **Lesson 15 (Bringing It All Together):** Pitch sheet is the foundation of your final project; it must be polished because it's your first impression to publishers.
- **Lesson 16 (Board Game Pitch Presentations):** Pitch presentations expand the pitch sheet verbally; the sheet must stand alone when the designer isn't in the room.

## Application
- **When pitching to publishers:** Lead with the one-page pitch sheet. They'll ask deeper questions if interested. Your answers to "Who plays this?" and "What's special?" determine if they request a prototype.

- **When pitching at conventions/events:** Have your pitch sheet printed. Use it as a leave-behind after your verbal pitch. This reinforces your message and gives them something to share internally.

- **When refining your game in development:** The pitch sheet serves as a clarity checkpoint. If you can't write it clearly, your game probably isn't clear enough to play.

- **Tradeoff: Simplicity vs. Detail** → A one-pager forces brutal prioritization. You cannot include everything. Choose: is your game's core appeal mechanical innovation, theme, or accessibility? Lead with that. Everything else is supporting detail.

- **Tradeoff: Art vs. Text** → Sample art or mood board increases impact but can distract. If your art isn't professional, use clean typography and smart layout instead. One excellent image beats five amateur attempts.

- **Example:** A strategy game pitch sheet might emphasize "Quick rounds, deep decisions" and include difficulty/solo play ratings. A family game might feature larger fonts and highlight "30-minute playtime" + "ages 8+." Same game, customized format.
