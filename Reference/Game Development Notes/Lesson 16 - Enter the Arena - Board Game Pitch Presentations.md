# Lesson 16: Board Game Pitch Presentations

## TL;DR
- Budgeting common error: ×7 multiplier without shipped costs. Use ×5 baseline; accept lower margin on first run if needed.
- Typical KS: 1,000 copies realistic, >5,000 is top 1%. Plan 1,000-2,000 for first indie game.
- What makes effective pitches: clear positioning (who plays + what's special), genuine enthusiasm, structured delivery, mechanical depth balanced with accessibility, market awareness
- What kills pitches: too much mechanical detail too early, vague positioning, lack of passion, market ignorance, over-committing to estimate numbers
- Pitch skill: Know your three main points. Come prepared, not improvised. Acknowledge early-stage work upfront.
- Memorable moments: Aim for one spotlight moment per player per session. Frequency scales with game length. Discover moments through playtesting.
- Original mechanics: Context matters. Execution + presentation can outweigh mechanical novelty (Wingspan). But offer *something* special—mechanic or execution.
- What separates good from great pitch: clarity of vision + genuine passion + market understanding.

## Core Concepts
- **Pitch Presentation as Extended Pitch Sheet** — The verbal pitch expands on the written one-page sheet. You can add context, nuance, and enthusiasm that a page cannot convey. But the core message (Who/What/How You Win) should be identical between written and verbal. Inconsistency signals confused thinking.

- **Clear Positioning as Gating Factor** — If listeners don't immediately understand who plays your game and why it's special, nothing else matters. Positioning (genre, player archetype, core appeal) must come before mechanics or story. "Who plays this and why?" is the first question answered, not a detail revealed later.

- **Genuine Enthusiasm as Signal** — Passion is contagious. If you believe in your game, listeners believe. Flat delivery kills even great game ideas. But enthusiasm must be genuine—over-the-top salesmanship reads as insecurity. Corey's feedback: "If you believe in it, I'm more likely to believe in it."

- **Mechanical Detail Paradox** — Deep mechanical explanation is tempting (especially for designers who've spent months on systems). But listeners process mechanics through play experience, not through explanation. Lead with moments/results, then explain the mechanics that create them. "You get dramatic showdowns" (moment) before "simultaneous action selection" (mechanic).

- **Memorable Moments as Experience Design** — Players retain moments (rolling three 6s, clutch defense, clever combo) better than rules. Aim for one spotlight moment per player per session. Frequency scales: 1-hour game needs fewer moments than 8-hour game. Moments can be engineered (card combos) or emergent (player creativity). Discover through playtesting.

## Design Principles
- If your pitch doesn't answer "Who plays this?" in the first 30 seconds → start over with positioning
- If your pitch is all mechanics with no moments → listeners won't remember or care; reframe around experience
- If you lack genuine passion for your game → don't pitch yet; maybe the game isn't ready or isn't for you
- If your positioning is defensive ("it's not like X, it's different") → reframe positively ("it combines Y and Z to create...") 
- If you've only designed mechanics and haven't playtested memorable moments → acknowledge it's early stage; focus on concept
- If budgeting has ×7 multiplier without shipped costs → recalculate with ×5; accept lower margin on first run
- If your print run estimate is >5,000 as indie first game → conservative thinking, but justify if possible; publishers expect 5,000+

## Heuristics (Fast Decision Rules)
- **The Three Main Points Rule:** You should be able to state three main pitch points in <30 seconds. If you can't, your pitch isn't focused enough.
- **The Enthusiasm Check:** Record yourself pitching. Do you sound genuinely excited? If you sound flat or over-rehearsed, you need to reconnect with why you designed this game.
- **The Positioning Test:** Before any mechanics, can you answer "Who plays this game and why?" in one sentence? If not, your positioning needs work.
- **The Moment Memory Test:** After playtesting, ask "What would you tell someone about this game?" If they describe moments (not rules), your game is working.
- **The Early Stage Honesty:** If your prototype is rough or mechanics are unfinished, say so first. Frame as "early concept" and focus on vision, not execution.
- **The Estimate Humility:** Frame MSRP as "based on estimates, could be $X depending on publisher margins" not "will retail for $X." Estimates change.

## Design Frameworks
- **The Five-Minute Pitch Structure:**
  1. Opening hook (15 sec): Who plays this? What's the core appeal? (e.g., "Deadman's Hand is a poker bluffer for players who love risk and theme-heavy Western games")
  2. The experience (90 sec): What memorable moments define this game? Lead with one spotlight moment. Why is it compelling?
  3. Mechanics + Production (90 sec): How do mechanics create that experience? Components needed? Production feasibility?
  4. Market + You (60 sec): What players want this? Competitive landscape? Why you're the person to make this?
  5. Closing passion statement (30 sec): Why do you believe in this game? What do you want players to feel?

- **Pitch Presentation Checklist:**
  - Positioning clear (genre + player type + core appeal)?
  - Three main selling points identified and stated?
  - One spotlight moment described vividly?
  - Mechanics explained in terms of experience, not rules?
  - Market awareness demonstrated (know your audience, know competitors)?
  - Production estimates framed as estimates, not commitments?
  - Genuine passion audible/visible?
  - If early stage, acknowledged upfront?

- **Feedback Analysis Framework:**
  - Clarity feedback = positioning or core loop unclear → rewrite opening, test on unfamiliar listener
  - Mechanical feedback = mechanics confused or boring → simplify explanation, lead with moment instead
  - Market feedback = positioning vague or missing market awareness → research competitors, identify target player
  - Passion feedback = delivery flat → reconnect with original design intent, record yourself, iterate

## Common Pitfalls
- **Mechanical detail instead of positioning** → Why fails: Listeners don't understand who should play or why. How to fix: "Deadman's Hand is for poker fans who want narrative and characters" (positioning) before "simultaneous action selection with bluffing" (mechanic).

- **Over-committing to estimate numbers** → Why fails: Estimates change; publishers know this. Overstated confidence in estimates signals inexperience. How to fix: "Based on my budgeting, this could retail for $35-45 depending on publisher margins and print run" not "This will retail for $40."

- **Flat delivery despite good game** → Why fails: Enthusiasm is contagious; lack of it is too. Publishers invest in designer passion as much as game mechanics. How to fix: Record yourself pitching. Note where your voice drops, where you sound bored. Reconnect with why you love this game.

- **Pitching unfinished mechanics as finished** → Why fails: Gaps in core loop are exposed immediately in Q&A. How to fix: If mechanics are unfinished, say so upfront. "Core loop is tested; expansion mechanics still in development." Focus on what's solid.

- **Ignoring market positioning entirely** → Why fails: Publishers want to know where on the shelf your game lives. Vague positioning suggests you haven't researched. How to fix: Name 3-5 comparable games. Explain how yours is different/better. Show you know the market.

- **Treating memorable moments as bonus, not core design** → Why fails: Games are remembered for moments, not rules. If your game has no memorable moments, something's mechanically wrong. How to fix: Playtest. Ask "What would you tell someone?" If they describe rules not moments, redesign for more dramatic/memorable play states.

- **Leading with "this is original/new mechanics"** → Why fails: Originality is burden to prove; execution is easier sell. How to fix: Lead with positioning + moment + market, not novelty. Let judges decide if it's original after they understand the experience.

## Application
- **When preparing to pitch:** Write three main points on an index card. Know them cold. Can you pitch your game in 30 seconds using those three points? Then you're ready for the five-minute version.

- **When describing your game mechanically:** Translate game-design jargon first. "Worker placement" → "You send your workers to locations to gather resources." "Push-your-luck" → "You can keep rolling for more points, but if you bust, you lose everything." Plain language > jargon.

- **When asked "Is this mechanic original?":** Answer honestly. "It borrows simultaneous action selection from [game], but applies it to [theme] in a way I haven't seen before." Honesty + specificity > claiming originality you can't defend.

- **When describing memorable moments:** Be vivid. Not "players can combo their abilities" but "Imagine you've been losing all game, then you chain three powers together for a comeback moment and everyone at the table leans in." Moment > mechanic.

- **Tradeoff: Depth vs. Accessibility** → Hobby gamers appreciate mechanical depth. Casual players want accessible experience. If your game serves both, lead with accessibility, then show depth. "Easy to learn, endless strategic depth" > "Complex but worth learning."

- **Tradeoff: Early Stage vs. Polished Pitch** → Rough prototype + clear vision > polished prototype + unclear vision. If your game is early stage, acknowledge it (builds trust) and focus on concepts you're confident in.

- **Example Case Studies from Lesson:**
  - Deadman's Hand: Strong positioning ("asymmetric poker bluffer"), clear enthusiasm, well-structured headers, balanced depth + accessibility. Strength: designer's passion came through.
  - Pathwords: Polished, focused, understood market gap (word games alienate non-experts), addressed problem directly. Strength: market awareness signal.
  - Modern Fantasy Dungeon Crawler: Early stage, clear design challenge (Frosthaven's complexity = barrier), smart solution (map books reduce components). Weakness: core loop unclear, needed mechanical specificity.
