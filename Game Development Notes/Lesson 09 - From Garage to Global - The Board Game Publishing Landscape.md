# Lesson 09: The Board Game Publishing Landscape

## TL;DR
- 3,000+ games released yearly. Publishers are extremely picky and time-constrained.
- Small publishers (1-4) rarely accept submissions. Medium (5-19) may consider. Large (20+) have specialized departments but move slowly.
- Find publishers via Cardboard Edison directory or research catalogs matching your game's tone.
- Licensed IP is a red flag for most publishers—prove the concept generically first.
- Traditional pipeline: Pitch → Option (advance $5K-$10K, 4-6% royalty) → Development (6-12mo) → Production (6-12mo) → Total 1-3 years.
- Contract must include 3-year publication deadline or rights revert to you.
- Self-publishing offers control but requires running the full business; crowdfunding bypasses gatekeepers but most campaigns fail.
- Stores work with distributors, not individual publishers. Need proven success (campaign or sales track record) for distributor interest.

## Core Concepts
- **Publisher Size Categories** — Small = founder-driven, internal focus. Medium = had one hit, may expand, risk-taking. Large = specialized departments, slow processes, risk-averse. Size determines acceptance likelihood and timeline.
- **Internal vs. External Design** — Internal companies create all their own games. External companies live on submissions. Research which type matches your approach.
- **Royalty Advance** — Upfront payment ($5K-$10K typical) against future royalties. If game earns less than advance, you keep the advance but get no additional payments.
- **Royalty Rates** — Typically 4-6%, but calculation method matters (gross vs. profit). Different publishers, same percentage = different real value.
- **Distribution vs. Direct Sales** — Stores order from distributors (one catalog, one transaction). Self-publishers need distributor relationships or direct sales won't scale. Most FLGS won't order from individuals without massive crowdfunding success.
- **Proof of Concept** — Print-on-demand (Game Crafter) shows sales history, doesn't hurt future deals, proves the game works without huge upfront cost.

## Design Principles
- If licensed IP → prove concept generically first, then approach licensor
- If seeking publisher → research 2-4 publishers with matching catalog tones
- If self-publishing → treat manufacturing as long-term partnership; plan 12+ months from decision to customer hands
- If pitching → focus on game quality over designer credentials; publishers judge the game on the table
- Avoid assuming all publishers want the same thing → different sizes and philosophies; match carefully
- Prioritise design involvement clarity → specify approval rights in contract if you care about development evolution

## Heuristics (Fast Decision Rules)
- **Publisher Readiness**: Check Cardboard Edison first. If not listed, google "[Publisher Name] submissions" or research at conventions.
- **The License Question**: If your idea requires licensed IP, build the generic version first. Success = leverage for licensing talks.
- **Timeline Reality**: 2-3 years from pitch to shelf is normal, not exceptional. One game pitching, one in dev, one in production = three-game pipeline.
- **Royalty Math**: $5K advance at 5% royalty + $10 wholesale = 50¢ per unit. Game must sell 10,000 copies to reach advance threshold.
- **Self-Pub Threshold**: Print-on-demand viable up to 500 copies. Above that, factory. Below 500, PoD usually makes sense.

## Design Frameworks
- **Traditional Pipeline (4-step model)**:
  1. Pitch document (1 page) → email or in-person at convention
  2. Option to Publish contract (timeline, advance, royalties, designer involvement specified)
  3. Development by publisher (6-12 months, may not start immediately)
  4. Production & distribution (6-12 months printing + shipping)

- **Publisher Evaluation Matrix**:
  - Size (1-4, 5-19, 20+) → determines speed and risk tolerance
  - Design philosophy (internal vs. external) → do they accept submissions?
  - Catalog tone (family, strategy, party, etc.) → does your game fit?
  - Track record → have they released similar games successfully?

- **Self-Publishing vs. Pitch Decision Tree**:
  - Goal = run game company? → self-publish, build brand, expand gradually
  - Goal = create games, not manage business? → pitch to publishers
  - Want full control over every detail? → self-publish (requires business skills)
  - Want to focus purely on design? → pitch (publisher handles rest)

- **Distribution Reality Check**:
  - Crowdfunding success (>$100K) → distributors interested
  - Direct sales track record → distributors see viability
  - No proof of concept → PoD first, then approach distributors with sales data

## Common Pitfalls
- **License assumption** → Assuming licensed IP will help pitch. Fails because: adds complexity, licensing fees, publisher restrictions. Fix: Build generic version, prove it works, then approach licensor with success as proof.
- **Pitching too early** → Sending prototype-level game to publishers. Fails because: publisher sees risk, judges on incomplete gameplay. Fix: Keep playtesting until game is tight and exciting.
- **Designer credential focus** → New designers assuming brand name matters. Fails because: publishers judge the game, not the designer resume. Fix: Let the game speak; credentials are bonus, not requirement.
- **Skipping contract clarity** → Not specifying designer involvement or publication deadline. Fails because: misalignment on changes, game gets shelved indefinitely. Fix: Negotiate approval rights and 3-year publication deadline upfront.
- **Underestimating self-pub scope** → Thinking self-publishing = designing better. Fails because: requires legal, taxes, fulfillment, marketing, conventions, scaling decisions. Fix: Only self-publish if you want to run a business, not just design.
- **Expecting immediate distributor pickup** → Approaching distributors without proof of concept. Fails because: distributors want track record, >5K sales or successful campaign. Fix: Do PoD or crowdfunding first, then approach with numbers.

## Connections
- **Lesson 10 (Rulebooks)** → Clear rules aid publisher evaluation. Well-written rulebook is marketing asset when pitching.
- **Lesson 11 (Manufacturing)** → Understanding production timeline (5-9 months file→delivery) informs contract negotiations and release planning.
- **Lesson 12 (Marketing)** → Self-publishers must execute full marketing (influencers, conventions, BGG). Publishers handle some but not all.
- **Crowdfunding** → Alternative to both publisher and self-pub; requires marketing capability of self-pub but funding comes from campaign.

## Application
- **For pitching**: Research Cardboard Edison monthly. Identify 3-5 matching publishers. Attend 1-2 conventions per year (Gen Con, UK Games Expo, Spiel Essen) with polished prototype. Build relationships over years, not one email.
- **For self-publishing** (decision point): Can you handle business administration, supply chain, customer service, and marketing alongside design? If yes, self-pub. If no, pitch. PoD is hybrid—low risk way to test market.
- **Designer credentials myth**: Focus on game quality. If you're a first-time designer, that's fine—judge only on the game. Build credentials through published games, then use them for second/third pitch.
- **Multiple game strategy**: Spread pitches across different publishers. Don't expect one game to launch a career. Plan 2-3 games simultaneously in pipeline (pitching, in development, in production).
- **Tradeoff—Publisher vs. Self-Pub**: Publisher = slower, less control, smaller upfront cost, royalties ongoing. Self-pub = faster, full control, high upfront cost, all risk on you.

## Source Notes (Condensed)
- Board game market: 3,000+ games/year with 4+ reviews on BGG (9+ per day).
- Cardboard Edison: ~$2/month for directory of actively accepting publishers.
- Advance range: $5K-$10K typical, varies by publisher and game.
- Royalty rates: 4-6%, but calculation (gross vs. profit) affects real value. SAZ and TTGDA provide contract examples.
- Publication deadline: 3 years standard; reversion clause essential.
- Print-on-demand: doesn't hurt future deals, shows proof of concept.
- Success threshold: 5,000 copies/year = successful. 10,000+ = strong. Most games sell <5K.
- Designer credentials: Less important than game quality when pitching.
