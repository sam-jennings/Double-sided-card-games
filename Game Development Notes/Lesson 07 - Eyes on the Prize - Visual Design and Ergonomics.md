# Visual Design and Ergonomics

## TL;DR
- Visual hierarchy (order eye is drawn to info) determined by font size, colour, position, visual weight. Rule of thumb: bold 10% of content, everything stands out; bold everything, nothing stands out.
- Never rely on colour alone; always include secondary indicators (icons, patterns, text) for colourblindness accommodation
- If text won't fit without shrinking excessively → redesign the card, not the font. Minimum 8pt font for readability.
- Natural reading order: top-left most important, bottom-right least (often obscured when cards fanned). Plan information placement accordingly.
- Localization: German words average 12 letters vs. English 5. Leave 3-5 blank lines on cards. Never put text on dice/tokens. Use black plate replacement for efficient multi-language printing.
- Token sizing: 18mm minimum, 4-5mm thick. Anything smaller is hard to pick up; less than 4mm feels too thin.
- Include one standout component (the "guile") for visual impact and free marketing
- Custom card sizes cost more; stick to standard (poker, bridge, mini) if budget-tight

## Core Concepts

- **Visual Hierarchy** — The order player's eye is drawn to information. Determined by font size, colour, position, visual weight. Intentional hierarchy guides players to what matters most.

- **Natural Reading Order** — Top-left most important, top-right important, bottom-left slightly important, bottom-right least important (often hidden when fanned). Cultural variation exists but this is general guideline.

- **Secondary Indicators** — Always pair colour with icon, pattern, or text. Colourblindness is common; colour alone excludes players.

- **Typography Principle** — If text won't fit without shrinking excessively, redesign the card. The problem is card design, not font size. 8pt minimum; larger is better.

- **Localization Burden** — Languages vary wildly in word length (English 5 letters avg, German 12). Leave 3-5 blank lines for translation. Black plate replacement (print text plate separately per language) makes multi-language production efficient.

- **Token Ergonomics** — 18mm minimum (roughly penny-sized). 4-5mm thickness. Anything smaller is hard to manipulate; thinner feels cheap.

- **Component Materials** — Wood (simple shapes, nice feel, expensive), Plastic (complex shapes, tooling cost), Cardboard (cheapest, least impressive).

## Design Principles

- If players overlook critical information → increase visual hierarchy (make larger, bolder, higher on card)
- If card text is hard to read → redesign card or layout, not font size (8pt is already minimum)
- If game will have international versions → leave 3-5 blank lines for localization, never put text on dice/tokens
- Avoid relying on colour alone for differentiation → always add icon, pattern, or text
- If component feels cheap → check thickness (4-5mm is standard); material choice affects cost more than appearance
- Prioritise playability over thematic appearance — readability first, theme enhances what's functional
- If pitching to publishers → impressive prototype influences their production choices; establish hierarchy and component choices that matter to you

## Heuristics (Fast Decision Rules)

- Top-left = most important info on cards (natural reading order)

- Bold 10% of content = everything stands out; bold everything = nothing stands out

## Design Frameworks

**Visual Hierarchy Framework**:
1. Identify information players need most frequently
2. Place at top-left (natural reading order)
3. Secondary information below/right
4. Least critical at bottom-right
5. Emphasise critical information (bolder, larger, higher position)
6. Test: what does eye land on first without instruction?

**Colorblindness Accommodation Framework**:
1. Never use colour alone for differentiation
2. Pair colour with icon, pattern, text, or symbol
3. Test visual appearance with colorblind simulator (Photoshop, Illustrator, online tools)
4. Example: Seven Wonders uses both colours and icons for card types
5. Remember: not just red-green; many types of colourblindness exist

**Icon Design Framework**:
- Distinctive silhouettes (vary shapes; not all circular, not all boxes)
- Works at small scale (test icons at actual size)
- Single-colour functionality (recognizable in black on cards, white on dice, any colour)
- Avoid multicolour (problematic in localization)
- Text primary, icons secondary reinforcement
- Icons work best on dice/tokens (where text can't fit) or as reinforcement

**Typography Best Practices**:
1. Minimum 8pt font (larger preferred)
2. Avoid illegible fonts (ornate medieval scripts fail at small size)
3. Fix widows/orphans (single words on line = hard to read)
4. Ensure contrast (dark text on dark background fails)
5. Avoid coloured text (localization costs multiple printing plates)
6. Consistency across cards of same type

**Localization Design Framework**:
1. Use black plate replacement (print text separately per language)
2. Leave 3-5 blank lines on cards for translated text
3. Average German word = 12 letters (English = 5)
4. Never put text on dice/tokens (too expensive to produce per language)
5. Test with translated versions to catch space issues
6. Black text costs globally; coloured text requires separate plates per language

**Material & Component Framework**:
- Wood: simple shapes, nice feel, expensive, printing costly
- Plastic: complex shapes, tooling cost upfront, rare printing
- Cardboard: cheapest, least impressive tactile feel
- Token sizing: 18mm minimum (penny-sized), 4-5mm thickness standard
- One standout component (the "guile") for visual impact and marketing

**Custom Component Considerations**:
- Standard card sizes (poker, bridge, mini) = no custom die cost
- Custom card sizes = setup cost; stick to standard if budget-tight
- 3D printing requirement = excludes large audience; only viable for passion projects
- Specialty items (petri dishes, cords, magnets) = factory conversations; they have supplier connections

## Common Pitfalls

- **Relying on colour alone**: Colourblind players can't differentiate → always add secondary indicator (icon, pattern, text)
- **Poor visual hierarchy**: Everything emphasised equally → nothing stands out; bold only critical information
- **Illegible fonts**: Ornate medieval script looks thematic but unreadable at small size → prioritise readability
- **Text at bottom-right**: Players fan cards in hand and that information disappears → place critical info top-left
- **Shrinking font instead of redesigning**: 6pt text is unreadable → redesign card if text won't fit at 8pt+
- **Thematic appearance over playability**: Beautiful ornate design that's hard to read/use → playability always wins
- **No space for localization**: Design assumes English word length → leave 3-5 blank lines for translation
- **Text on dice/tokens**: Each language requires separate production → use icons instead for international games
- **Custom card size without budget**: Unique sizes require custom dies → use standard (poker, bridge, mini) if cost-conscious
- **3D printing requirement**: Excludes most players without 3D printers → only viable for passion projects

## Application

**When establishing visual hierarchy on cards**: Start with: what information do players need most frequently? Put that at top-left, larger, bolder. Secondary info goes below or right. Least critical goes bottom-right. Test without instructions: where does eye land first?

**When designing for colourblindness**: Never use colour alone. Add icon (different shape), pattern (stripes vs. solid), or text label. Test with colorblind simulator. Example: if red and green differentiate card types, add also add square vs. circle icons.

**When dealing with small text**: If text won't fit at 8pt without shrinking further, redesign the card. Maybe the card has too much information. Maybe text needs to go elsewhere. The issue is the card design, not the font size.

**When planning international versions**: Leave 3-5 blank text lines on cards (languages vary in length). Never put text on dice or tokens (too expensive per language). Use black plate replacement for efficient printing (all cards print without text, then text prints separately per language).

**When choosing component materials**: Wood feels nice but limits shape complexity and adds printing costs. Plastic enables complex shapes but requires tooling cost. Cardboard is cheapest but feels cheap. Match material to your visual targets and budget.

**When sizing tokens**: 18mm minimum (roughly penny-sized). 4-5mm thick. Anything smaller is hard to pick up; anything thinner feels cheap. Test with actual players to confirm comfort.

**When pitching to publishers**: The prototype you show influences their production choices. Establish visual hierarchy and component choices that matter to you; this influences their art direction and material choices.

**Tradeoff**: Ornate medieval script looks thematic but unreadable at small size. Clean sans-serif font is readable but less atmospheric. Readability always wins; use thematic fonts where possible (headers, larger text) but prioritise legibility for game-critical information.
