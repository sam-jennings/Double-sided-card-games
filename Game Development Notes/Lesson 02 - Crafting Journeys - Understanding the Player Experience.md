# Lesson 02: Understanding the Player Experience

## TL;DR
- Four player types (Family, Social, Strategic, Thematic) exist; most players are blends, not pure types
- Players have three primary motivations: To Win, To Have Fun, To Socialise (+ Tell Stories)
- Euro games (mechanics-first, elegant, low randomness) and American games (theme-first, chaotic, high randomness) represent different philosophies; modern games blend both
- Engagement = identify your core tenant (what you're optimizing for) and maximize it relentlessly
- Design accessibility to your target audience: rulebook length, font size, turn complexity should match audience expectations
- Can't appeal to incompatible motivations (direct conflict + no-confrontation); choose your focus
- Semi-cooperative games historically fail because they disappoint both cooperative AND competitive players
- Magic Circle concept: Games define spaces where normal social rules don't apply (lying in Werewolf is required, not rude)

## Core Concepts

- **Gamer Types** — Framework for understanding what players want (not strict categories):
  - Family: Quality time together (Ticket to Ride, Zombie Kids Evolution)
  - Social/Party: Experience > winning; memorable moments (Codenames, Just One, Werewolf)
  - Strategic: Deep decisions, optimization, puzzle-solving (Brass, Terraforming Mars, Wingspan)
  - Thematic: Story and narrative first (Cthulhu: Death May Die)
  - Most players blend these; Twilight Imperium appeals to all four simultaneously

- **Player Motivations** — Why they keep playing:
  - **To Win** — Competitive focus; optimize strategy; tournament-oriented
  - **To Have Fun** — Experience and escapism matter more than winning; joy of moment
  - **To Socialise** — Face-to-face connection; tabletop-specific (video games lack this); secondary motivation is telling shared stories

- **Engagement** — Measurable indicator of why players return. Comes from identifying your game's core strength and maximizing that specific appeal rather than trying to do everything well.

- **Magic Circle** — The agreed-upon space where normal social rules are suspended. Werewolf example: lying is required, expected, and doesn't break trust because players agree it's part of the game. Designer must define magic circle clearly (what's allowed, what's not).

- **Accessibility Triad** (interconnected):
  - Rulebook length should match audience complexity expectations
  - Font size and card text density should match reader skill level
  - Turn structure complexity should match mental load tolerance

- **Euro vs. American Design Philosophies** — Historical categorization; modern games increasingly blend:
  - **Euro (Mechanics-First)**: Mechanics before theme; puzzly; indirect player interaction; low randomness; elegant systems; minimize elimination (Carcassonne, Catan, Ticket to Ride)
  - **American (Theme-First)**: Theme before mechanics; chaotic; direct conflict; high randomness; simulationist; accept player elimination (Gears of War, Cosmic Encounter historically)
  - **Hybrid/Modern**: Both traditions blended; thematic games with elegant mechanics; direct interaction in strategic games (Twilight Imperium 3rd Edition case study: added action cards for elegance while preserving theme)

## Design Principles

- If optimizing for multiple incompatible motivations → choose one focus instead
- If designing semi-cooperative (work together but some eliminate) → expect historical failure rate; separate into fully cooperative OR fully competitive
- If targeting family players → max 4-page rulebook, readable fonts, simple turn structure
- If targeting strategic players → more rulebook depth acceptable; complex turn phases tolerable
- If using thematic language/terminology → accept it confuses new players; optimize for veteran immersion over newcomer clarity (or find balance point)
- If designing accessibility → account for colorblindness, font readability; physical mobility harder (requires lived experience on team)
- If using directness of conflict → ensure it aligns with theme (cooperative games shouldn't have direct PvP unless thematic)

## Heuristics (Fast Decision Rules)

- **Audience match gut-check**: Does rulebook complexity match my target? Simple > Family, Complex > Strategic
- **Conflict coherence**: Is my game type (co-op/competitive) aligned with theme? If not, choose differently
- **Magic Circle clarity**: Can players instantly understand what's "allowed" in the game? If not, state it explicitly
- **Tactile appeal**: High-quality components (wooden tiles vs. cardboard) measurably improve engagement for casual players
- **Exception count**: If players constantly ask "wait, can I do that?" during playtests, too many rule exceptions
- **Core tenant test**: Can I describe in one sentence what this game optimizes for? If no, unclear focus

## Design Frameworks

### Player Type to Game Design Mapping
| Player Type | Wants | Optimize For | Examples | Design Elements |
|-------------|-------|--------------|----------|-----------------|
| Family | Quality time, accessible rules | Simplicity, repeatable plays | Ticket to Ride, Carcassonne | 1-4 page rulebook, large fonts, intuitive turns |
| Social | Memorable experiences, bonding | Memorable moments, laughter, conversation space | Codenames, Werewolf, Just One | Light rules, social deduction mechanics, bluffing |
| Strategic | Deep decisions, optimization puzzle | Strategic depth, multiple viable paths | Brass, Terraforming Mars | Complex systems, player choice, asymmetric starts |
| Thematic | Narrative and atmosphere | Story immersion, character consistency | Cthulhu: Death May Die, Mansions of Madness | Flavor text, thematic mechanics, narrative arcs |

### Accessibility Tuning Framework
**For your target audience, adjust:**
- **Rulebook**: Family 1-4 pgs double-sided | Casual 4-8 pgs | Hobbyist 10-20 pgs | Complex 20-40+ pgs
- **Font Size**: Family 14pt+ | Casual 12pt | Hobbyist 10pt | Complex 9pt acceptable
- **Card Text Density**: Family sparse, simple | Casual moderate, clear | Hobbyist detailed | Complex dense okay
- **Turn Phases**: Family 2-4 simple steps | Casual 4-6 steps | Hobbyist 6-8 detailed | Complex 8+ with sub-steps
- **Rule Exceptions**: Family minimize | Casual few | Hobbyist moderate | Complex accept more

### Magic Circle Definition Template
- **What's allowed**: [behaviors normal social rules forbid but game explicitly permits]
- **Why it's allowed**: [thematic reason or rule framework]
- **What breaks magic circle**: [behaviors that violate the agreement]
- Example (Werewolf): Allowed = lying about role; Why = deduction game; Breaks = lying about game state (how many votes)

### Design Choice Decision Matrix: Can Your Game Appeal to Both?
| Motivation Pair | Compatible? | Resolution |
|-----------------|------------|-----------|
| To Win + To Have Fun | Yes | Design skill expression that's also enjoyable |
| To Socialise + Strategic Depth | Yes | Minimize turn downtime; keep all engaged |
| Direct Conflict + No Confrontation | No | Choose one, design accordingly |
| Competitive + Full Cooperation | No | Choose one; semi-cooperative fails historically |
| Thematic Role-Play + Min-Maxing | Partial | Use mechanics to discourage min-maxing, not theme |

## Common Pitfalls

- **Designing for "everyone"** → Impossible; different player types have incompatible preferences. Semi-cooperative games fail because they disappoint both camps. Fix: Choose your core audience and optimize for them relentlessly.

- **Rulebook complexity doesn't match audience** → Family games with rulebook length of Twilight Imperium alienate newcomers. Complex games with over-simplified rules frustrate veterans. Fix: Audit rulebook length against target player complexity tolerance.

- **Thematic language creates accessibility barrier** → Android: Netrunner uses cyber terminology (stack, R&D, running) that immerses veterans but confuses newcomers. Fix: Decide whether to prioritize thematic immersion or mechanical clarity; find conscious tradeoff point.

- **Components undermine audience** → Tiny fonts, dark backgrounds, small tokens alienate families. Over-simple components disappoint strategic players. Fix: Match component quality to audience expectations.

- **Misaligned game type and theme** → Competitive game about cooperatively solving a mystery breaks immersion; cooperative game with individual victory conditions feels hollow. Fix: Align game structure (co-op/competitive) with thematic narrative.

- **Magic Circle isn't clear** → Players uncomfortable with lying in deduction game or unsure whether mean plays are okay. Fix: Explicitly define what social rules are suspended in your game's magic circle.

- **Turn downtime kills social motivation** → Strategic games with high downtime between player turns push social players away. Fix: Design simultaneous actions or keep turns snappy for inclusive social experience.

## Application

**When defining your target audience:**
1. Name your primary player type (Family / Social / Strategic / Thematic or blend)
2. Identify their primary motivation (To Win / To Have Fun / To Socialise)
3. Determine one core appeal you're optimizing for (what will keep them coming back?)
4. Design accessibility elements to match: rulebook length, font size, turn complexity, rule exception count
5. Define magic circle: what social rules does your game suspend?
6. Audit components: do they match audience quality expectations?

**Testing accessibility with playtests:**
- Can newcomers understand rules from rulebook alone?
- Do players constantly ask "can I do that?" (too many exceptions)
- Do all players stay engaged during others' turns?
- Does thematic language help immersion or create confusion?

**When choosing between incompatible motivations:**
Don't try both. Example: Semi-cooperative game that tempts players to betray each other disappoints cooperative players (wanted pure co-op) and competitive players (wanted full PvP). Instead: pick full co-op OR full competitive, then design accordingly.

**Tradeoff example — Thematic Language:**
- Prioritize immersion: Use specific cyberpunk terminology (Android: Netrunner); accept newcomer confusion; design teaching module
- Prioritize clarity: Use generic mechanics language; add flavor text in sidebars for thematic immersion; easier onboarding
