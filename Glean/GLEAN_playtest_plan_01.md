# GLEAN — Playtest Plan 01

**Game:** GLEAN v1.0  
**Player count:** 3  
**Players:** Designer + 2 parents (experienced trick-takers, new to this deck)  
**Symbol subset:** Full 9-symbol / 36-card deck (12 cards each at 3P)  
**Variant progression:** Plunder (game 1) → Full majority scoring (game 2+)  
**Date:** June 2026

---

## Purpose

First table test of GLEAN. Played in the same session as TURNOVER — the players will already understand double-faced cards and the flip action. This tests whether the trick engine (follow by either face, hidden face wins) is teachable to people who already know trick-taking, and whether the accumulation goal ("win tricks to score") lands naturally.

Starting with Plunder (sum pips of captured cards) isolates the trick engine from the majority-scoring layer. If Plunder works, the majority game is introduced in game 2.

---

## Hypotheses to test

1. **"Follow-by-either-face is learnable within 2–3 tricks."**  
   Prediction: players who already know "must follow suit" will map "follow the led sign" quickly. The flip-to-follow move clicks because they've been flipping cards in TURNOVER.  
   Failure signal: repeated illegal sloughs, or players not realising a card in their hand *can* follow because the led sign is on its other face.

2. **"The simultaneous reveal creates a dramatic beat."**  
   Prediction: flipping all cards at once produces a moment — "who hid the highest?" Players react to the winner being determined by the hidden face.  
   Failure signal: the reveal feels like admin. Players just want to know who won and move on.

3. **"Players feel meaningful choice in which follower to play."**  
   Prediction: at least one player deliberates between two legal plays — recognising that different cards hide different strengths. The "spend your high hidden card" decision surfaces.  
   Failure signal: players grab the first card that matches without considering alternatives.

4. **"Plunder scoring is instant and satisfying."**  
   Prediction: at round-end, players flip through their harvest, sum pips on both faces, and have a score in under a minute. The goal is clear throughout ("I want to win tricks with high-value cards in them").  
   Failure signal: scoring is slow or confusing ("wait, do I count both faces?"), or players don't feel connected to the score during play.

5. **"The majority layer adds depth without overwhelming."** (game 2 only)  
   Prediction: after one Plunder round, players can handle "whoever has the most cards of a sign claims its pip value." They can count to 4–5 per sign across 12 cards.  
   Failure signal: counting nine majorities at round-end takes more than 2 minutes, or players can't track what they're competing for during play.

6. **"A 3P round completes in 10–12 minutes."**  
   Prediction: 12 tricks at ~30–60 seconds each once learned. First Plunder round ~12 min with teaching; subsequent rounds ~8 min.  
   Failure signal: round drags past 15 minutes due to AP or confusion.

---

## What to watch

- **Following mechanics.** Can players scan their hand and find a legal follow quickly? Do they naturally flip a card to show the led sign, or hesitate?
- **The choice of follower.** Do players look at what they're hiding before choosing? Or just show the led sign and hope?
- **Leading decisions.** Does the leader think about *which* sign to lead and *which* face to show? Or just play whatever's on top of their hand?
- **The reveal.** What's the emotional temperature? Suspense? Satisfaction? Indifference?
- **Harvest awareness.** Do players glance at their (or others') harvests during play? Do they track who's winning what, or only count at the end?
- **Card counting.** Does anyone notice "there are 8 cards with Crown on them — I've already seen 5"?
- **Sloughing.** When void, do players choose deliberately what to throw in (high-value gift to the winner) or slough randomly?
- **Plunder → majority transition.** If you play both, what's the learning curve on round 2? Does the majority concept land from a verbal explanation, or need a worked example?
- **Energy.** Are players still engaged after TURNOVER + a round of GLEAN? Note when to stop.

## Teaching plan

**Frame it** (~30 sec): "This is a trick-taking game — like Whist or Bridge, you play cards in turn and someone wins the trick. The twist: what wins isn't what you show. You all show the same sign to follow; what wins is what's *hiding* on the other side."

### The trick engine (~3 min)

1. **Recap the deck** (~15 sec). "Same deck. Each card has two signs, one per face, and a pip number 1–9 in the corner. Higher pip = stronger."

2. **Demo a lead** (~30 sec). Play a card from your hand choosing a face. "I lead by showing this sign — that's the led sign. Everyone must follow it."

3. **Demo following** (~1 min). Have each parent play a card carrying the led sign. "You must show the led sign. If it's on the other face, flip the card. If you genuinely don't have one at all, play anything — but you can't win." Point out: *everyone* who follows is showing the same symbol. So the shown face can't decide anything.

4. **Demo the reveal** (~30 sec). "Now we all flip. The highest pip among the followers wins. Look — mine was a 7, yours was a 3, yours was an 8. You win the trick." Emphasise: ties are impossible (every pair is unique, so every hidden face in a followed trick is a different sign with a different pip).

5. **Demo the harvest** (~30 sec). Winner takes all cards into their harvest spread. "These are yours now. Both faces count for you."

### Plunder scoring (~30 sec)

"For now, simple scoring: at the end, every card in your harvest scores the sum of its two pips. So a card with a 4 and a 7 scores 11 for you. Most total points wins. Win tricks with fat cards."

### Play 2–3 open tricks, then start for real.

**Defer:** majority scoring (introduce before game 2 if you get there), The Passing, match format.

---

## Session plan

- **Game 1 (Plunder, 1 round):** Full trick engine, simplified scoring. Focus on whether follow-by-either-face works and whether winning tricks feels good.
- **Game 2 (full majority scoring, 1 round):** Introduce "whoever has the most cards carrying a sign claims its pip value." Walk through scoring together after the round. See if it feels richer or just heavier.
- **Game 3 (if energy allows):** A second majority round with a different starting leader. Or revert to Plunder if majorities didn't land.

A full match (3 rounds) is probably too much for a first session following TURNOVER. Play what the table wants.

---

## Transitioning from Plunder to full scoring (between games 1 and 2)

"OK, new scoring this time. Instead of summing pips, you're competing for *signs*. Each of the 9 signs appears on 8 cards in the deck — whoever captures the most cards carrying a sign *claims* it and scores its pip value. So if you gather 5 cards with Crown on them and I only have 3, you claim Crown and score 9 points. Ties mean nobody gets it. You're fighting nine little battles at once — and every card you capture helps you in *two* of them, because it carries two signs."

Do a worked example with 3–4 cards to show how one card counts for two majorities.

---

## After-session questions

- Was following by either face confusing or natural?
- Did you feel like you chose *which* card to play, or just played whatever matched?
- Did the reveal (flipping to see who won) feel exciting?
- (After majority scoring) Was counting the nine signs at the end OK, or tedious?
- Did you feel like you knew what you were competing for during play, or only found out at the end?
- Would you play again? Plunder or the full game?

---

## Success criteria

The session is a success if:
- Both parents follow legally (flip to show led sign) without reminders after trick 3–4.
- At least one player makes a deliberate follower choice ("I'll play this one because it hides a higher pip").
- The reveal generates a reaction at least once per round.
- Plunder scoring takes under a minute.
- If majority scoring is tried: players can identify who won at least 5/9 signs without help.
- Players can explain the basic strategy ("hide your strong cards for the tricks that matter").

The session raises a red flag if:
- Follow-by-either-face is still confusing after 5+ tricks (the engine doesn't teach itself).
- Players feel no agency — "I just play whatever matches and hope my hidden face is high/low."
- The reveal is ignored — players just want to know who won and move on.
- Majority scoring takes more than 3 minutes or produces visible frustration.
- Players are confused about what both faces of a card "do" (which is the one you show vs. the one that wins vs. the one that scores).
