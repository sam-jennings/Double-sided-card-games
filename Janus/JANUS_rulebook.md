# JANUS

*A co-operative game of half-seen things for 2–4 players · ~20 minutes · ages 10+*

Janus, god of doorways, has two faces — and so does every card in your hand. Hold your fan and you see one face of each card; the rest of the table sees the other. **Nobody may know both faces of a live card in a player's fan** — except through legal deduction or an omen. (Keystones and archive cards are shown openly; the secret lives only in the fans.) Together, you must raise the god's gates: keystones locked in place by pairs of pillars with a **matching buried foundation**. You know what your pillars show *you*. Only your friends know what they show the world.

---

## Components

- **21 cards**: the 7-symbol sub-deck of the master deck (remove every card showing symbol 8 or 9 on either face; every pair of the remaining 7 symbols appears on exactly one card).
- 6 tokens or coins for **omens** (any 6 small objects).

## The one sacred rule

> **Never look at the outward face of a card in your fan.** Pick cards up without turning them, hold the fan steady, and place cards without peeking. You see your side; the table sees theirs. That asymmetry *is* the game.

## Setup

1. Shuffle the deck, flipping random chunks so faces are randomised.
2. Reveal **4 gates** (3 for the apprentice game, 5 for masters): draw four cards one at a time, show **both sides** to everyone, announce the pair aloud ("this gate is Flame-and-Moon"), and place them in a row, either face up. These are **keystones**; their pairs are public knowledge for the whole game.
3. **Validate the gates.** Count how often each symbol appears across all the keystones. In the 7-symbol game, **no symbol may appear on 4 or more keystones** — if one does, the setup is mathematically unwinnable; reshuffle and reveal new gates. (General rule for an *n*-symbol deck: reject the setup if any symbol appears on more than **floor((n − 1) / 2)** keystones — so max 3 at 7 symbols, max 4 at 9 symbols.)
4. Deal each player a fan — **2P: 6 cards · 3P: 4 · 4P: 3** — dealt sliding flat off the deck so nobody flips anything, **outward face down against the table** so the future owner never glimpses it. Pick your fan up seeing only your side. The rest (5 cards at standard) is the **deck**, kept in a face-down stack — **no card's face is public until it enters a fan or the archive**.
5. Place **4 omen tokens** in the middle (2 in reserve). Whoever most recently walked through a doorway goes first.

## Your turn — one action

On your turn, choose **exactly one** of the three actions below. "Costs no omen" / "spend 1 omen" describes the omen price of that action — it does not mean the action is extra or free of charge against your turn.

### 1. Read an omen *(spend 1 omen token)*

Point to **all** cards in one teammate's fan that show the same symbol *to the table*, and name it: "These two show Crown to us." 

For the receiver this is lightning: they already know their side of each pointed card, and every card is a unique pair — so each pointed card is now **fully identified** to them. (And every *un*-pointed card is now known *not* to hide Crown.)

### 2. Raise a pillar *(costs no omen)*

Each keystone has two open sides, one per symbol of its pair. Take a card from your fan and place it on an open side, **showing the keystone's symbol for that side**, declaring first whether you are playing it:

- **"My face"** — you place it with your side up. Always legal if your side shows the right symbol. You now *lose sight* of its other face — which the whole table has been staring at all game.
- **"Your face"** — you place it with the *outward* side up, betting (on hints, memory, deduction) that it shows the right symbol. If the revealed face is wrong: **fumble** — take a strike, card to the archive.

**The lock-check.** The moment a keystone has both pillars, flip them: if their two buried faces **match each other**, the gate locks — the three cards form a perfect triangle of symbols. Lock the set, **regain 1 omen** (max 6). If the buried faces differ: **strike**, the keystone stays open, and **each mismatched pillar returns to its owner's fan** (held normally, your side to you, outward face to the table). The lock-check has now shown everyone both faces of those two cards — that knowledge stays on the table, as all knowledge does in Janus — but the cards themselves are back in play and may be raised again. Returning the pillars instead of archiving them keeps a gate's only possible foundations available: a failed lock costs you a strike and tempo, not the cards you still need.

### 3. Consult the archive *(costs no omen; regain 1 omen)*

Discard any card from your fan to the **archive**, where cards lie open to all — anyone may flip archive cards at any time. Discarding tells the table the truth about one of your cards, forever.

**Refilling.** When an action removes a card from your fan — **raising a pillar** or **consulting the archive** — refill by drawing one card from the deck **if the deck is not empty**. Take the top card without looking at its face: slide it into your fan so its outward face points at the table, and lift the fan seeing only your secret side. **Reading an omen does not remove a card, so you do not refill after it.** A pillar that returns to your fan from a failed lock is not a draw and triggers no refill.

## Winning and losing

- **Win:** all gates locked.
- **Lose:** the third **strike**, or the **final round** ends with any gate still open.
- **The final round (exact timing):** the moment a player refills with the **last card of the deck**, they finish their current turn as normal. Then each *other* player, going clockwise, takes **one** final turn (they may take any action, including reading omens; there is simply no card left to draw, so they do not refill). After the last of these turns, score the board: all gates locked is a win, otherwise a loss. The player who emptied the deck does **not** get an extra turn beyond the one they were taking.
- Brag by omens remaining; the perfect game locks every gate with zero strikes.

## Why the lock works (30-second tour)

A keystone Flame/Moon needs pillars Flame/X and Moon/X — *the same X* — to form the triangle Flame-Moon-X. The pillar you played "my face" up showing Flame has a buried X that *you* can't see but your teammates can remember; the Moon pillar's buried face is someone else's same problem in mirror. Every pair exists exactly once, so the archive, the locked gates, the keystones, and every omen reading all chip away at what each buried face *can possibly be*.

## What you may and may not say

Talk is the heart of Janus — deduce out loud, argue, plan. But because half the table can see what a cardholder cannot, communication needs a hard wall, or the game collapses into one person reading another's cards aloud.

**You may freely discuss:** your own visible faces, the public keystones, locked gates, archive cards, the omen count, whose turn it is, and any logical deduction drawn from that shared information ("Flame-Star can only be founded on Crown now — the other Crowns are gone").

**You may not** give advice, confirmation, denial, reaction, hint, or any card-specific recommendation that is based on a live face you can see but the cardholder cannot. The *only* channel for that information is a formal **omen reading**.

Illegal, even though none names a hidden symbol outright:

- "Don't play that one." / "Not the left card."
- "Your second card is useful for the Moon gate."
- "I'd play your left card as *your face*." / "Trust me, flip that one."
- "Would anyone object if I play this to Flame?" (fishing for a reaction)
- Wincing, hesitating, or going quiet when a teammate reaches for a card.

Legal:

- "By my count every Moon foundation except Crown is dead." (pure deduction from open info)
- "I'm out of safe plays, so I'll consult the archive." (about your own choice)
- "Spend an omen on me — I think a reading would unlock a lot." (asking for an action, not leaking a face)

When in doubt: if the sentence would change meaning depending on a face only *you* can see, don't say it — pay an omen instead.

## Edge cases & FAQ

**Can one player raise both pillars of a gate?** Yes — usually as a deliberate gamble, since they can't see either buried face. The table watching both backs may be screaming inside. Let them scream silently.

**Can X equal another keystone's symbol?** Yes. Any of the 7 symbols can be a foundation except the keystone's own two (a pillar showing Flame can't bury Flame — it's one card).

**Both pillars are placed and one was a fumble?** The fumble strike resolves first; the lock-check never happens — the surviving pillar stays, waiting for a new partner.

**Does an omen reading expire?** No. Knowledge is forever; fans never reshuffle. But a *drawn* card enters the fan after readings — track what was pointed at.

**Can I read an omen about my own fan?** No. Janus does not answer prayers about yourself.

**We're out of omens and nobody can safely play.** Consult the archive — discarding is always legal and pays an omen. The economy is built to never deadlock.

**Exact deal arithmetic.** 21 cards − 4 keystones = 17; fans take 12 (all player counts); deck = 5. Apprentice (3 gates): deck 6. Masters (5 gates): deck 4.

## Strategy notes (read after your first game)

- **The first pillar should usually be "my face".** It's safe to place, and it hands the *deduction* to the people who can see what you buried — your teammates' next omen or discard can tell the Moon-side player whether their candidate matches.
- **Discards are messages.** Consulting the archive with the *right* card both pays an omen and proves a pair dead. The strongest discard answers a question nobody could ask aloud.
- **Count what's gone.** Each symbol lives on exactly 6 cards. Keystones and locked gates retire pairs forever — late game, buried faces are often deducible to certainty with no omens at all.
- **"Your face" plays are the endgame.** Early they're coin flips; after two readings and a busy archive they're the team's precision tool — and the only way to use a card whose *your-side* symbol is useless.

## Variants

- **Apprentice:** 3 gates, table-talk fully open (still never describe a visible face). Teach with this.
- **Masters:** 5 gates, start with 3 omens.
- **The Oracle (2P):** one player may not speak at all; the other may only ask yes/no questions. Omens work normally. Eerie, excellent on a quiet evening.
- **Ninefold Temple (hard campaign):** play with all 9 symbols / 36 cards, 6 gates, fans 2P: 8 / 3P: 6 / 4P: 5 (deck 14 / 12 / 10). Longer deductions, heavier memory — for tables that have locked many gates.

## Changelog

**v1.1** — structural and clarity fixes from design review:

- **Setup validation (fixes unwinnable openings).** Added the frequency cap: no symbol on more than floor((n − 1)/2) keystones (max 3 at 7 symbols). Brute force confirms ~1.75% of random 4-gate setups and ~8% of 5-gate setups were previously unwinnable before anyone moved; the cap is a provably exact filter — it rejects all and only the unsolvable openings.
- **Removed the public deck-top.** A public top face plus gaining the secret on draw let a player know both faces of a live card, breaking the core rule. The deck is now a face-down stack; a card's face becomes public only on entering a fan or the archive. Added a dealing/refill handling protocol so holders never glimpse their outward face.
- **Dead-card safeguard.** A failed lock now returns both pillars to their owners' fans instead of the archive, so a gate's only possible foundations can't be destroyed mid-game (archiving and fumbles could previously make a "solvable" board unwinnable).
- **Action wording.** "Free" → "costs no omen," with an explicit one-action statement. Refill now happens only when a card actually leaves the fan (not after an omen reading).
- **Communication.** Added a strict section with legal/illegal table-talk examples to prevent indirect leaking and quarterbacking.
- **Final-round timing** defined exactly.
- **Intro wording** corrected: "nobody sees a whole card" → "nobody may know both faces of a *live* card," consistent with open keystones and archive.

**Suggested playtest ramp:** 3 gates / 4 omens, then 4 gates / 4 omens, then tune omens and strike count.
