# THE COUNCIL

*A game of reversible promises for 3–6 players · ~25 minutes · ages 12+*

Issues come before the council; the council talks, promises, commits — and then, one by one, in full view, the members may turn their coats. Every card you commit supports exactly one cause and can defect to exactly one other, and everyone can read both options off your card. The richest issues go to whoever stands **alone** — so the whole game is a negotiation about who gets to be lonely.

---

## Components

- The **36-card** master deck. The corner **index pips (1–9)** give each symbol its value — a Sun issue (9) is worth more than a Void issue (1).

## Setup

1. Shuffle thoroughly, flipping random chunks.
2. Deal each player a hand of **4** (examine both sides freely, always).
3. The rest is the **issue deck** (3P: 24 · 4P: 20 · 5P: 16 · 6P: 12 — at six players it empties exactly).
4. Choose a first **Speaker** (whoever last changed their mind in public). The Speaker token (any object) rotates left each round.

The game lasts **4 rounds** — one per hand card. Every card will be spent.

## A round

### 1. Issues are tabled

Flip **3 cards** from the issue deck to the middle, one at a time. Each card's **visible symbol** is an open issue, worth its pip. Stack any new issue on other tabled cards of the same symbol — stacks of unresolved issues grow, and stacks are won whole.

*(Both faces of an issue card are shown to everyone as it's flipped, then it lies issue-side up. Like all public cards in this collection, tabled issues may be re-examined by anyone at any time.)*

### 2. The council talks

Open negotiation, any promises, any lies. Nothing said is binding. Nothing ever is.

### 3. Commitment

Everyone secretly chooses one hand card and holds it **flat between their palms**. When all are ready, the Speaker counts down and everyone **slaps their card down together**, chosen face up.

Your card **supports** the tabled issue matching its visible symbol. A card showing a symbol with no tabled issue is an **abstention**.

### 4. The turncoat phase

In seat order starting left of the Speaker, each player may — once — **flip their committed card** to its other face, defecting to the issue matching the new face (or into abstention, or out of it). Everyone always knew your card's only alternative: the threat was public the moment you slapped it down. Late seats defect with better information; the Speaker rotation shares that power around.

### 5. Resolution

For each symbol with tabled issues:

- **Exactly one supporter** → they take that symbol's entire issue stack. Carried alone.
- **Two or more supporters** → deadlock. The stack stays on the table and grows next round.
- **No supporters** → the stack stays.

All committed cards then go face-up (as they finished) to a public discard row.

## Game end & scoring

After round 4, unclaimed issue stacks rot unresolved. Each player sums the **pips of the issue cards they carried**. Highest total wins. Tiebreak: most issue cards; then the player who carried an issue **most recently**.

## Why it bites

With three issues and more than three players, somebody must clash, abstain, or be talked into the cheap seats — and the 9-pip stack everyone refused to share keeps rolling over, getting heavier. You can promise the Sun and flip to the Moon, but only to the Moon: your treachery is printed on your card, and the council prices it accordingly.

## Edge cases & FAQ

**Two issues of the same symbol tabled in one round?** Same stack. One lone supporter takes it all.

**Can I support a stack that rolled over from a previous round, even if it wasn't flipped this round?** Yes — any tabled symbol is an open issue.

**Everyone abstains?** Everything rolls over. The council yawns.

**Can the turncoat flip be declined?** Of course. Most threats die unexecuted; that's what makes them threats.

**My committed card's two symbols match two different tabled issues.** Enviable. You choose at commitment, and may still defect to the other in the turncoat phase.

**Does the Speaker break deadlocks?** No. Deadlocks are the point.

**Six players, round 4:** the issue deck runs out exactly. At fewer players the spare cards are never seen — which keeps the table's knowledge of unflipped pairs honest (you can't count what was never dealt).

## Strategy notes (read after your first session)

- **Value the stacks, not the round.** A deadlocked 9 returns next round worth the same 9 — but with fewer total commitments left in hands, loneliness gets easier to arrange. Sometimes the play is to *cause* the deadlock you intend to harvest in round 4.
- **Abstention is a weapon.** Showing a dead symbol while holding a live partner threatens entry-by-flip into any adjacent fight, for free, in the turncoat phase. The scariest councillor is the one apparently doing nothing.
- **Count seats before promising.** Your flip happens in a fixed order this round. A promise you can't physically honour by the time your turn comes (because the defection you'd flee is later than you) is a promise you should sell dearly.
- **The discard row remembers.** All spent cards are public. By round 3 the table knows most of what's left in hands — bluff accordingly, and count what your rivals *must* be holding.

## Variants

- **Backroom (5–6P):** after commitment but before the turncoat phase, one minute of open table talk. More knives.
- **The Whip (gentler):** deadlocked stacks are *discarded*, not rolled over. Shorter, tactical, less explosive round 4s.
- **Long Parliament:** play two full games, second with reversed seat order; sum scores. Cancels any seat advantage the Speaker rotation leaves behind.

---

*Design v1.0 — untested at the table. Core design choices: lone-supporter-takes-all turns the game into an asymmetric minority game (the degenerate "everyone takes one issue each" collusion at 3P is broken by unequal pip values and rollover stacks); the palms-commit protocol replaces the face-down concealment this deck cannot have; all spent material stays public (no memory). First playtest questions: does 4 rounds feel one short; do deadlock stacks roll often enough to matter; is the Speaker rotation enough to balance flip order at 5–6P.*
