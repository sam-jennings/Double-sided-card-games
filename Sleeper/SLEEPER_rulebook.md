# SLEEPER

*A game of hidden loyalty for 4–6 players · ~25 minutes · ages 12+*

Every courtier at this table wears two faces. The sign you show is your public oath; the one you keep pressed to the wood is the master you truly serve. Steer the Court's decrees toward your secret master's sign, read the two faces of everyone around you, and choose your moment to turn your coat in the open. When the Court rises, everyone sworn to the **ruling sign** wins together — if you can work out who that is.

---

## Components

- The **36-card** master deck: 36 double-faced cards, 9 signs, every pair of signs together on exactly one card, each sign on exactly **8** cards. The corner **pips (1–9)** rank the signs — Crown (9) outranks Moon (1).
- *(Optional)* one **Steward** token — any small object — to mark the first player.

No subset needed: SLEEPER uses all nine signs at every player count.

## The two faces

A card lying flat on the table shows **one** sign to the whole table; its other sign is hidden only because it is pressed to the wood. There is no card back. This single fact is the game: your loyalty sits face-up in plain sight, and the only thing anyone *cannot* see is the sign you have turned downward.

## Setup

1. **Shuffle** thoroughly, flipping random chunks of the deck as you go so that which face lands up is random.
2. **Swear your loyalty.** Deal each player **one** card, taken **flat between the palms** and read in private. Look at both of its signs. Secretly choose one to be your **Master** — the sign you truly serve — and lay the card flat in front of you with your **Master face down** and the other sign, your **cover**, showing. You chose your master from the two you were dealt; the other is the oath you fly in public, true or not.
   - *This is the only secret in the game.* Keep your loyalty card flat and pressed. You may glance at your own Master, but never let another player see it. Everything else on the table is public and may be re-examined by anyone at any time.
3. **Open the Court.** Stack the rest of the deck as the **Court deck**. Reveal its top **3** cards in a row in the middle — this is the **Court**, the realm's standing decrees.
4. **Choose a first Steward** (whoever last kept a secret, or any method). The Steward token passes one seat to the left each round.

## The Court row

The **Court** is a face-up row. Each card's **showing sign** is a decree in force, and a sign's **strength** is simply how many Court cards currently show it. The row records itself: every push and every betrayal stays readable at a glance, so nothing has to be remembered.

## A round

Beginning left of the Steward and going clockwise, each player takes **one** turn. On your turn, **reveal the top card of the Court deck** for all to see, then do **one** of:

| Action | What you do |
|---|---|
| **Decree** | Add the revealed card to the Court row, **the face of your choice up.** You strengthen that sign by one. |
| **Turn a coat** | Set the revealed card aside on the **spent pile**, and instead **flip one card already in the Court row** to its other face. That decree swings from the sign it showed to the one printed on its back — and to no other. |

Either way, **one card leaves the Court deck every turn** — it is the game's clock.

Once per game, at the very start of any of your turns, you may also **Unmask**: flip **your own** loyalty card so your Master shows, permanently. You have proven beyond any bluff exactly whom you serve. Unmasking is free (you still take your normal action this turn), and an unmasked loyalty can never be hidden again.

Talk freely throughout — promise support, trade accusations, name a sign you will back, lie about your master. Nothing said is binding.

The round ends when every player has taken a turn. Pass the Steward token one seat left and begin the next round.

> **A coat turned (worked example).** The Court row shows Crown · Leaf · Crown — Crown is strength 2. On your turn you reveal a card off the deck; you would rather weaken Crown than add to the row. You **Turn a coat**: the revealed card goes to the spent pile, and you flip the rightmost **Crown** card. Its back is **Wave**, so that decree is now Wave. The row reads Crown · Leaf · Wave — Crown has dropped to strength 1, Wave is on the board, and the whole table watched exactly which two signs your betrayal moved between. A flip can only ever send a decree to the *one* sign printed on its back, and that back was public the moment the card hit the table.

## Game end & the reckoning

The game ends when the **Court deck is empty.** Finish the go-around in progress, then hold the reckoning:

1. **The ruling sign.** Count the showing signs across the whole Court row. The sign on the **most** cards **rules the realm.** *Tie:* among the tied signs, the one with the **higher pip** rules (Crown over Moon).
2. **Unmask everyone.** All players turn their loyalty cards Master-up.
3. **Win together.** Every player whose **Master is the ruling sign wins.** Everyone else loses — however hard they worked, they served the wrong crown.

It is a shared victory: there may be one winner, several, or — if no one swore to the sign that rose — none at all.

## Why it bites

You can only win by making your master rule, but the instant you *openly* push one sign, the table reads your loyalty and starts turning your decrees over. So you push through others: talk a rival into strengthening your sign for their own reasons, hide inside a crowd already backing it, and keep your master downturned until the count is safe. Every flip is a public, exact swing between two named signs, so a betrayal is never a surprise in *direction* — only in *timing*. And because anyone sworn to the winner wins, your fiercest opponent across the table might be your secret ally. You just can't both admit it.

## Edge cases & FAQ

**May I change my Master mid-game?** No. Your loyalty is locked at setup. The only thing you can ever do to your loyalty card is **Unmask** it; you can never re-choose or re-hide it.

**Both signs on my dealt card looked good.** Serve one, in private, at setup. The other is your cover.

**Can two players share a Master?** Yes — most signs sit on eight cards, so several players may secretly serve the same one. They win together, though they cannot be certain of each other until the reckoning.

**Why would I ever Unmask?** To be believed. A player who *proves* they serve Crown can credibly rally the table to strengthen Crown — but now everyone knows to stop them unless Crown is their master too. Reveal to lead; stay hidden to scheme.

**Can a decree be flipped back and forth forever?** No — turning a coat costs you the card you revealed that turn, and the Court deck is finite, so flips are strictly limited. The row settles as the deck runs down.

**The card I revealed is one I would rather not place at all.** You can always spend it on the spent pile by Turning a coat instead of Decreeing. You are never forced to strengthen a sign you dislike — but you then add no strength of your own that turn.

**Nobody serves the ruling sign — really no winner?** Really. The realm crowned a sign no courtier truly served, and everyone goes home empty. Rare, and entirely fair: you collectively failed to steer the Court onto anyone's master.

**Can I lie about my master all game?** Always — lying is free and your cover supports it. The catch is that talk is cheap: only the **Court row** decides the winner, so a beautiful lie that never moves a card wins nothing.

## Strategy notes (read after your first game)

- **Hide in the crowd.** The safest sign to serve is one several players have their own reasons to back. Let them build it; add your quiet decree late.
- **Read the flippers, not the talkers.** Whoever keeps turning decrees *toward* a sign is likely sworn to it — or wants you to suspect a rival. Watch what people protect.
- **The back of a decree is a threat.** A Crown card with Leaf on its back is a Crown decree that becomes a Leaf decree at a touch. Read the row by what it *could* become, not only what it shows.
- **Unmasking is a lever, not a confession.** Time it to swing a hesitant table late, when there is no longer room to punish you for it.
- **Choose your master for the whole game, not the deal.** You are locked to whichever of your two signs you pick at setup, so pick the one you can plausibly *crown* — strength on the board beats sentiment.

## Designer's variants

- **The Wager (adds a deduction payoff).** Just before the reckoning, each player secretly notes in their palm one other player and the sign they believe that player serves. Reveal during the reckoning: a correct read lets you settle the ruling-sign tie your way, and counts as a consolation half-win if your own master did not rule. Rewards reading people, not only steering cards.
- **Two Masters (harder, 5–6P).** Keep *both* of your dealt signs live: you win if **either** rules — but you may only ever Unmask to prove one of them. More hedging, more webs.
- **Open Court (gentler first game).** Play one practice round with all loyalties face-up, so newcomers see how decrees, flips, and the reckoning connect before secrecy is switched on.

*Design v0.1 — untested at the table. Core choices: the single guarded loyalty card is the only hidden information — the one persistent secret a backless deck can hold cleanly, since a flat card hides exactly its down face; steering a shared public Court row replaces any hidden hand; every turn spends one Court-deck card, so the game is guaranteed to end; flips are exact, public, one-alternative swings (the deck's reversible-commitment property); and victory by shared secret alignment keeps the table's real teams invisible until the reckoning. First playtest questions: can players guard one flat loyalty card all game without leaks (JANUS could not); does emergent shared-master alignment create satisfying alliances or kingmaking when several serve the winner; does the everyone-picks-the-obvious-sign line need a balance fix (as THE COUNCIL's lone-supporter collusion did); and — the load-bearing question — do players actually deduce masters from decree-and-flip patterns, or does the Court row become noise?*
